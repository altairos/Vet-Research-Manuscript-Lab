INTRODUCTION

Canine lymphoma is one of the most common hematopoietic tumors in
dogs, accounting for approximately 7-24% of all canine cancers [1].
The CHOP-based chemotherapy protocol (cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin,
vincristine, and prednisone) has been the standard of care for
decades, with reported median survival times ranging from 6 to 12
months depending on protocol variations and patient characteristics.

Several factors influence treatment outcomes, including clinical
stage, immunophenotype (B-cell vs T-cell), substage (a vs b), and
presence of hypercalcemia [2,3]. Recent studies have also explored
the role of breed-specific predispositions and molecular markers
in predicting treatment response [4].

However, most published studies suffer from small sample sizes,
single-institution bias, and inconsistent reporting of censoring
patterns. The STROBE-Vet guidelines were developed to address
these reporting gaps in veterinary observational studies [5].

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Study Population

This retrospective study included all canine patients diagnosed
with multicentric lymphoma at the Synthetic Veterinary Teaching
Hospital between January 2018 and December 2023. Patients were
included if they received at least one cycle of CHOP chemotherapy
and had complete medical records including follow-up data.
